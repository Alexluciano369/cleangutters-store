Section 1: Headline Formulas (50+ Patterns)

Pattern 1: The "How To" Headline

How to Retire at 45 With $2.5 Million — Even If You Earn Under $100K
Source: Personal finance newsletter, 350K subscribers
🔍 Why it works: Specific outcome ($2.5M at 45) + specific objection handled ("even if under $100K"). The specificity signals credibility; the objection-handling expands the audience. Pattern: "How to [specific result] — even if [common barrier]."
How I Built a $50K/Month Business Working 20 Hours a Week
Source: SaaS founder Twitter thread, 2.4M impressions
🔍 Why it works: First-person + specific revenue + desirable lifestyle hook. "How I" is more intimate than "How to." The juxtaposition of high income + low hours triggers the "what's the secret?" curiosity loop.

Pattern 2: The List Headline

7 Pricing Tactics That Increased Our Revenue 340% (One Is Free to Implement Today)
Source: B2B SaaS blog, #1 on Hacker News
🔍 Why it works: Odd numbers outperform even numbers. "340%" is specific (not "300%"). The parenthetical hook ("one is free") creates a "which one?" curiosity gap that drives clicks.

Pattern 3: The Contrarian Headline

Why Your "Growth Mindset" Is Actually Holding You Back
Source: Psychology Today, 12K shares
🔍 Why it works: Attacks sacred cow. People who identify with "growth mindset" feel a defensive urge to click and argue, or a curiosity to see if they're the exception. Pattern: "Why [widely accepted thing] is [negative outcome]."

Section 2: Email Subject Lines

Can I ask you something? (takes 10 seconds)
Source: Ramit Sethi, 50%+ open rate
🔍 Why it works: Personal + curiosity + low-friction promise. The "(takes 10 seconds)" defuses the "I don't have time" objection before they finish reading the subject line.
You're not going to like this email
Source: Copyhackers, 45% open rate
🔍 Why it works: Reverse psychology. People instinctively open emails that suggest they might disagree with the content. The pattern works because it signals "this isn't fluff — this is real talk."

Section 4: Landing Page Frameworks

PAS (Problem-Agitate-Solution)

Problem: "Most freelancers spend 40% of their time on admin work instead of billable hours."

Agitate: "That's 16 hours a week. $1,600 in lost revenue. $83,200 a year. Gone. To sending invoices and chasing clients."

Solution: "FreelanceFlow automates every admin task — invoicing, contracts, follow-ups — in one click. The average user saves 12 hours in their first week."

🔍 Why: The agitate step is where most copy fails. Notice the escalation: 40% → 16 hours → $1,600/week → $83K/year. Each step makes the pain more concrete and costly.

AIDA (Attention-Interest-Desire-Action)

Attention: "The average SaaS churn rate is 5% monthly. Top performers are under 2%."

Interest: "The difference isn't product quality — it's onboarding. Users who complete guided setup are 4x more likely to still be paying at month 6."

Desire: "OnboardKit gives you the exact same onboarding flows used by Stripe and Notion. Interactive tours, checklists, and contextual help — installed in 5 minutes."

Action: "Start your free trial. 3-minute setup. No credit card."

Section 5: CTA Button Copy That Converts

Weak CTAStrong CTAWhy
"Submit""Get My Free Guide"Reinforces what they get; "Submit" is about you
"Buy Now""Start Growing Today"Describes outcome, not transaction
"Sign Up""Try It Free — 30 Seconds"Reduces perceived friction
"Learn More""See How It Works"More specific, suggests demonstration
The CTA Test: Can the user picture exactly what happens after they click? If not, rewrite it. "Get My Free Guide" → they picture a download. "Submit" → they picture... nothing. The difference in conversion is often 20-40%.
