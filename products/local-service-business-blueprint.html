Chapter 1The One-Call Close: How I Book 60% of Inbound Calls

78% of service business jobs go to the company that answers the phone first. Not the cheapest. Not the best. The first. I tracked this for years. The math is brutal: if you're not answering on the first ring, you're already losing.

But answering fast is only half the game. The other half is closing on that first call — turning a "just getting a quote" into a "yes, come out tomorrow."

The script that closed 60%+ of calls

"Thanks for calling, this is Alex. … Got it — overflowing gutters on a two-story in Cherry Hill. I can have someone out tomorrow morning. It's $149 for a full clean and we'll flag anything that needs repair before it gets worse. Want me to lock that in? … Great — what's the best number to text you the confirmation?"

Notice every word is doing work:

  • "This is Alex" — personal, not a call center. Immediate trust.
  • Repeat their problem back — shows you listened, not just taking notes.
  • "Tomorrow morning" — specific time, not "sometime this week." Urgency without pressure.
  • State the price — don't make them ask. Hiding the price kills the momentum.
  • "Lock that in" — assumes the sale. Doesn't ask "would you like to book?" That's a question they can say no to.
  • End with the next step — the confirmation text. You're already doing the job in their mind.

What NOT to say

  • ❌ "Would you like to schedule an appointment?" — too formal, opens the door to "let me think about it."
  • ❌ "I'll have to check my schedule" — kills urgency. You're not a doctor's office.
  • ❌ "Our prices start at..." — vague pricing invites shopping around.
  • ❌ "Is this a good time to talk?" — you just answered their call. Of course it is.

Chapter 2Price Anchoring: Why I Charge $149 and Never Discount

The biggest closing mistake in trades businesses is competing on price. The cheapest contractor always loses — because the cheapest contractor always has the worst margins, the worst customers, and the worst reviews.

How I moved from $89 to $149 without losing customers

I didn't raise prices all at once. I tested three tiers:

TierPriceWhat's includedPick rate
Basic clean$149Clear gutters, bag debris, flush downspouts30%
Clean + inspect + minor repair$249Full clean + 20-point inspection + seal leaks55%
Full service$399+Clean + guards quote + roofline check + photos15%

The middle tier was the play. 55% of people picked it because it felt like the smart choice — not the cheapest (risky), not the most expensive (excessive). When you give three options, people compare your options to each other instead of comparing you to competitors. That's price anchoring, and it works.

The "never discount" rule

When someone asks for a discount, never say "sure, I'll knock $20 off." Instead say: "I hear you on the price. Here's what I can do — I'll add a free 20-point inspection on top of the clean so you know the full picture of your gutters. That normally costs $49 extra." You just protected your price AND made them feel like they won.

Chapter 3The Objection Playbook: Every "No" and How to Flip It

Over 50,000 jobs, I heard every objection in the book. Here are the ones that actually come up — and the exact words that turn them around.

"That's too expensive"

"I hear you. The other guys might be cheaper — but we're the highest-rated crew in the area for a reason. We don't leave until it's right, and if we find a problem, we flag it before it becomes a $4,000 repair. Is $149 worth knowing your roofline is clear?"

Why it works: Acknowledge, don't argue. Reframe the price as insurance. End with a question that makes them do the math themselves.

"I need to think about it"

"Totally fair. What specifically do you want to think through? If it's the timing, I can hold the spot for 24 hours. If it's the price, I can explain what's included so you know exactly what you're getting. What's your biggest hesitation?"

Why it works: "I need to think about it" is never the real objection. It's a polite way of saying something else. Your job is to find the real objection and address it directly.

"I'm going to get a few more quotes"

"Smart — you should always compare. Here's what I'd ask when you talk to the other guys: are they insured? Do they have reviews you can check? And do they guarantee the work? We're 4.9 stars across 847 reviews and we carry $2M in insurance. When you're ready, I'll have a spot."

Why it works: You just planted the criteria they'll use to judge everyone else. You set the bar. And you left the door open without being needy.

"Can you just do it cheaper / skip the inspection?"

"I could — and I'd be cutting corners on your house. The inspection is how we catch the leak that turns into a $4,000 fascia repair. It's $149 because I'm protecting your biggest investment, not just clearing leaves."

Why it works: You just reframed the upsell as protection. The customer feels taken care of, not sold to.

Chapter 4The Assumptive Close: Booking Without Asking Permission

The biggest leak in most service businesses is the soft ask — "would you like to book?" That question gives the customer permission to say no. The assumptive close removes that exit.

How it works

Instead of asking IF they want to book, you move forward as if they've already decided. You give them the next step, not a decision point.

"I've got tomorrow 9–11 or Thursday 1–3. Which works better for you?"

Not "would you like to schedule?" — "which time works?" They're choosing between slots, not between booking and not booking. The decision is already made.

The calendar close

When they say "let me check my calendar," you say: "No problem — I'll pencil you in for tomorrow at 10 and text you the confirmation. If anything changes, just text me back. No commitment." Now they have to actively cancel instead of passively say yes. Cancellation friction > booking friction.

Chapter 5Door-to-Door Closing: Getting the Yes Face-to-Face

I got my first 10 customers by knocking on 40 doors. Here's the script and the psychology.

The opening that works

"Hi, I'm Alex — I do gutter cleaning in the area. I noticed yours look like they've got some buildup. I can clear them out and check for leaks for $99. Want me to take a look while I'm here?"

Key moves:

  • Don't pitch. Observe. "I noticed yours look like they've got buildup" — you're not selling, you're helping. You saw something specific about THEIR house.
  • Low entry price. $99 is low enough to say yes on the spot. You upsell on the ladder once they've seen you work.
  • "While I'm here" — creates urgency without pressure. You're already in the neighborhood. The marginal cost of saying yes is zero.

The on-ladder upsell

"Your gutters are clear now — but I noticed a small leak at this joint. I can seal it right now for $49 or it'll get worse over the winter. Want me to handle it?"

They're watching you work. They trust you. The upsell feels like a recommendation from their mechanic, not a sales pitch. That's the best time to close additional revenue.

Chapter 6The Follow-Up Close: Recovering "I'll Think About It"

"I'll think about it" is where most service businesses lose 40% of their pipeline. The customer was interested, said they'd get back to you, and then life happened. Your competitor who followed up first got the job.

The 24-hour follow-up that recovered 30% of lost leads

"Hey [name], this is Alex from yesterday — hope the gutters are looking better. I had a slot open up tomorrow morning in your area. Want me to lock it in before someone else grabs it?"

Why this works:

  • Reference the specific interaction — not a generic "just following up."
  • Create real scarcity — "slot open up" means other people are booking. Not fake urgency.
  • Offer the next step immediately — "lock it in" is better than "are you still interested?"

Chapter 7Closing the Repeat: Why Retention Beats New Sales

New customer acquisition costs 5–7× more than retention. The cheapest revenue you'll ever get is from someone who already trusts you. Here's how I closed the repeat customer — without being pushy.

The seasonal re-book nudge

"Hey [name], it's been about a year since we cleaned your gutters. Fall is coming — want me to get you on the schedule before the rush? Reply C to confirm."

The "problem spotted" reactivation

"Re: the leak we flagged at your last visit — just wanted to check if you've had it looked at. If not, we can seal it on our next visit for $49. Better now than after the first snow."

This one closes at 40%+ because it references something they already know about and frames it as prevention, not upsell.

The referral close

"Glad you're happy with the work. Quick question — do you know any neighbors or friends who might need their gutters done? If you send them our way, I'll give you $25 off your next clean."

Referrals from happy customers close at 80%+ — because the trust transfers. One referral is worth ten cold leads.

Chapter 8Training Your Crew to Close (So You Can Stop Doing It)

You can't scale a business where you're the only closer. At some point, you have to train someone else to close — and that's where most owners fail. They either hire a "salesperson" who doesn't know the trade, or they hire a tradesperson who can't sell. You need both.

The close training checklist

  1. Shadow you for 20 calls. They listen. They don't talk. They hear the script, the rhythm, the objections.
  2. Role-play the top 5 objections. You play the customer. They practice the exact words. Until it's muscle memory.
  3. Give them a pricing sheet, not pricing discretion. Crew members should never negotiate. They quote the sheet. If the customer pushes, they say "let me check with the office" and call you.
  4. Tie their bonus to close rate AND reviews. If they get paid per job AND get a bonus for 5-star reviews, they'll close hard AND do great work. Those two incentives together are unstoppable.
The best closer in my business wasn't me — it was a guy I trained from scratch who had never sold anything in his life. He just learned the scripts, believed in the product, and followed the system. Closing is a skill. It's trainable. That's what this playbook is for.
