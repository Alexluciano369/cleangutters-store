Chapter 1Getting Your First 10 Customers in 7 Days

You do not need a website, a logo, business cards, or an ad budget to get your first customers. I got mine with a phone, a ladder, and a notepad. Here is the exact sequence I used — and that I'd use again today if I had to start over in a brand-new town.

Day 1–2: The neighborhood sweep

Pick one neighborhood — ideally one with mature trees and houses 15+ years old (those gutters are full). Knock on doors. Don't pitch. Say this:

"Hi, I'm Alex — I do gutter cleaning in the area. I noticed yours look like they've got some buildup. I can clear them out and check for leaks for $99. Want me to take a look while I'm here?"

Out of 40 doors, expect 25 answers, 8 conversations, and 2–3 yeses. That's a paying day from nothing. The trick: do the work in front of them, do it well, and ask for the next thing while you're on the ladder.

Day 3–4: Mine the people who already know you

Text every person in your phone. Not a blast — individual texts:

"Hey [name] — I'm running my own gutter cleaning business now. If your gutters need clearing before the next storm, I'll do yours for $79 (friends rate). Know anyone who needs it too?"

Half won't reply. A quarter will say "maybe later." And a few will say yes — and those few will refer you. Referrals from people who trust you close at 80%+. Cold doors close at 5%. Always start warm.

Day 5–7: Plant the flag online (free)

  • Google Business Profile — free, takes 20 minutes, and it's the single highest-ROI thing you'll ever do. This is where reviews live and where 70% of my calls came from for 15 years.
  • Nextdoor — claim your business page. Neighbors recommend trades to each other constantly. One genuine recommendation thread fed me jobs for months.
  • Facebook local groups — don't spam. Answer one "anyone know a good gutter guy?" post per week, helpfully, with a photo of recent work.

The real lesson

Your first 10 customers don't come from marketing. They come from showing up, doing honest work, and asking. Marketing is what you build after you have proof. Don't get this backwards — I see people spend $3,000 on a website before they've cleaned a single gutter.

Chapter 2Pricing So You Actually Make Money

The #1 reason service businesses fail isn't lack of customers. It's pricing too low to survive. I almost killed my business in year two by being "the cheap guy." Here's how pricing actually works.

Know your true cost per hour

Before you price anything, know what an hour of your time costs you to deliver. Add it up:

CostExample (monthly)
Truck payment + gas + insurance$900
Tools, ladders, equipment wear$200
Phone, software, Google ads$300
Your own pay (you are NOT free)$5,000
Liability insurance$250
Total$6,650

If you work 120 billable hours a month, you need to net $55/hour just to break even on your own salary — before profit. Most beginners charge $40 and wonder why they're broke and exhausted.

The pricing rule that changed everything

Price for the value and the risk, not for the time. A gutter clean isn't "two hours of labor." It's "I climb a 30-foot ladder so you never have to, and I catch the leak before it rots your fascia." That's worth $149, not $89.

I moved my base price from $89 to $149 over two years. I lost the cheapest 20% of customers — the ones who called three companies for the lowest quote and were never loyal anyway. My revenue went up and my headaches went down.

Always offer three tiers

  • Basic clean — $149. Clear, bag, flush downspouts.
  • Clean + inspection + minor repair — $249. Most people pick this. It's the anchor.
  • Full service: clean + guards quote + roofline check — $399+. The 10% who pick this fund your month.

When you give one price, people compare you to competitors. When you give three, people compare your options to each other — and pick the middle. That's not a trick; it's giving people a clear, honest choice.

Chapter 3The Phone Is Your Cash Register

For 15 years, the single biggest difference between a good month and a great month was this: answer the phone on the first ring.

When a homeowner's gutter is overflowing in a storm, they call five companies. Whoever picks up first usually gets the job — before the other four even return the voicemail. I once tracked it: 78% of jobs went to the first company that answered live.

The 3-ring rule

If you can't answer within three rings, you're losing money. Options, in order of preference:

  1. Answer it yourself, every time, even on the ladder.
  2. Hire a part-time person or answering service the moment you can afford it.
  3. At minimum, set up an instant text-back: "Thanks for calling The Trades Guy — I'm on a roof right now, I'll call you in 15 min. What's your address?"

The booking script that closed 60%+ of my calls

"Thanks for calling, this is Alex. … Got it — overflowing gutters on a two-story, in Cherry Hill. I can have someone out [tomorrow morning / this afternoon]. It's $149 for a full clean and we'll flag anything that needs repair before it gets worse. Want me to lock that in? … Great, what's the best number to text you the confirmation?"

Notice: I name the price, I create urgency honestly (storm damage gets worse), I assume the booking, and I capture the contact. Don't end a call without a scheduled date or a follow-up time. "I'll think about it" is where money dies.

Chapter 4Hiring (and Firing) Crews Without Going Broke

I fired three crews before I learned how to build one that lasted. Here's what those expensive mistakes taught me.

Hire for character, train for skill

Anyone can be taught to clean a gutter safely in a week. Nobody can be taught to show up on time, treat a customer's property with respect, and not cut corners when you're not watching. Hire the reliable person, not the experienced one.

The mistakes that cost me

  • Crew #1: Hired fast because I was drowning in jobs. Two of them no-showed during peak season and left customers furious. Lesson: never hire desperate.
  • Crew #2: Paid hourly with no accountability. They stretched a 90-minute job into a 4-hour day. Lesson: pay per job, not per hour, once they're trained.
  • Crew #3: Skipped reference checks. One had a habit of "finding" expensive repairs that didn't exist. It nearly torched my reputation. Lesson: your name is on every job — protect it ruthlessly.

The pay structure that worked

Base per-job rate + a quarterly bonus tied to review score and repeat-customer rate. This aligns the crew with the only two things that grow a service business: doing great work and getting people to come back. A guy making a bonus for 5-star reviews treats every customer like gold.

Chapter 5The Review Engine That Built My Business

847 reviews at 4.9 stars wasn't luck. It was a system I ran on every single job. (This is the same system in the standalone 847-Review Playbook — here's the core of it.)

  1. Ask at peak happiness. The moment the customer sees their clean gutters and clear downspouts — that's the high. Ask then, in person: "If you were happy with the work, a quick Google review genuinely helps a small business like mine — would you mind?"
  2. Make it one tap. Text them the direct review link before you leave the driveway. A link they have to search for never gets used.
  3. Respond to every review, good or bad. A thoughtful reply to a 5-star review tells the next reader you care. A calm, fix-it reply to a 1-star review sells more than the 5-stars do.
  4. Turn reviews into ammunition. Screenshot the best ones. Put them on your truck, your Google profile, your texts. Social proof compounds.

The number that matters

Reviews aren't vanity. For 15 years, my Google rating was the #1 factor in whether a cold lead called me or the other guy. Going from 4.6 to 4.9 measurably increased my call volume. Treat your review score like the asset it is.

Chapter 6Scheduling, Routing & Not Losing Your Mind

Once you have steady jobs, the enemy becomes chaos: driving 40 minutes between jobs, double-booking, forgetting to confirm. Here's the operating rhythm that kept four states running.

  • Batch by geography. Group jobs by zip code and only book a given area on a given day. I'd run all of Cherry Hill on Mondays, Marlton/Medford Tuesdays, and so on. Cut my windshield time in half.
  • Confirm the day before, every time. A simple text: "Confirming your gutter clean tomorrow between 9–11am. Reply C to confirm." No-shows dropped from ~15% to under 3%.
  • Build in buffer. A two-story with clogged downspouts always takes longer than you think. Book 4–5 quality jobs a day, not 8 rushed ones. Rushed jobs become refunds and 1-star reviews.
  • One source of truth. Whether it's a $20/mo app or a shared calendar, everything lives in one place the whole team sees. Sticky notes on the dashboard kill businesses.

Chapter 7Cash Flow, Taxes & the Numbers That Matter

Plenty of "busy" service businesses go under. Being busy isn't being profitable. Watch these numbers, not just your bank balance.

NumberWhy it matters
Revenue per jobAre you raising prices over time, or stuck?
Jobs per day per crewYour true capacity and utilization.
Repeat-customer rateThe cheapest revenue you'll ever get. Guard it.
Cost to acquire a customerIf a Google lead costs $40 and a job nets $90, scale ads. If not, fix the funnel first.

The tax discipline that saved me

  • Open a separate business account on day one. Never mix personal and business money. It makes taxes and sanity possible.
  • Set aside 25–30% of every payment for taxes the moment it lands. The IRS does not accept "I spent it" as an answer.
  • Track every mileage and every receipt. Trucks, fuel, equipment, insurance, software — these deductions are real money back. A $40/mo bookkeeping app pays for itself the first April.
  • Pay yourself a real wage, not just "whatever's left." A business that can't pay its owner isn't a business — it's a job that's slowly bankrupting you.

Chapter 8Scaling to Multiple Crews & Multiple States

Going from "me on a ladder" to "four states" wasn't one big leap — it was a hundred small systems. The principle: you can't scale chaos, only systems.

  1. Document everything you do before you hire anyone to do it. If the perfect job lives only in your head, you can never duplicate yourself. Write the checklist: how you arrive, clean, inspect, photograph, and close.
  2. Promote a lead before you add a crew. Every new crew needs someone accountable who isn't you. Grow your best worker into a crew lead first.
  3. Standardize the customer experience so a job in New York feels identical to a job in Delaware. Same script, same pricing tiers, same review ask, same quality bar. Consistency is the brand.
  4. Expand to adjacent geography, not random territory. I grew along highways out of South Jersey — NJ, then PA, then DE, then NY. Each new area was a short drive from a proven one. Don't leapfrog into a market you can't service well.
  5. Protect the thing that got you here: answering the phone, honest pricing, real reviews. Most businesses lose their soul when they scale. The ones that win keep the day-one standards at 100x the volume.
There is no hack. There is no shortcut. There is no secret. There's a phone you answer, a price that's fair, work you're proud of, and a review you earned — repeated ten thousand times. That's the whole business. Now go build it.
